Name One Genius That Ain't Crazy: On Kanye West, Taylor Swish (I mean, "Swift"), and Media Representatio﻿n
By: Julia Leonardos
​
I don’t have to tell you that the Kanye West / Taylor Swift beef is among the best longstanding celeb feuds out there (right above Anthony Bourdain vs. Paula Deenand Azeala Banks vs. everyone).

I also don’t have to tell you that the West vs. Swift saga is Very Important. It drags issues of race, gender, and genre into the mainstream, and the beef itself is often indistinguishable from the media parlance it is couched in.

When the beef began, the outrage, across the nation, was palpable. Kanye long ago had been branded by the mainstream as an Egomaniacal and Impulsive Black Male Rapper thanks largely to his (truly brilliant) post-Katrina comments on live television. So when he, again on live television, interrupted Taylor Swift, a nineteen-year-old Innocent White Girl country crooner, during her acceptance speech for “Best Female Video” at the September 2009 VMA’s, the outrage came…well, swiftly.

Was what Kanye did at the 2009 VMAs rude? Uncalled-for? Cringe-worthy? Obviously.
Does that mean he was wrong? That it wasn’t a meaningful act of resistance against a system that rewards whiteness and remains terrified of Black sexuality, femininity, masculinity, and excellence? That he was not making an important statement? That he wasn’t aware that he was doing would not be perceived as “acceptable”? Of course not.

​It is easy to vilify Kanye, and ignore the larger issues of representation and opportunity that face mainstream Black musical artists. (An identity which itself is still, in 2016, shockingly close to oxymoron: see the white outrage following Beyoncé’s latest single and accompanying music video).

Kanye says and does what he wants, when he wants it. He’s eccentric. And he doesn’t really care what other people think. If you’re white and male, we think that’s inspiring. If you’re Black and anything, we think it’s scary.

Kanye West has spent years running up against a large piece of the mainstream media machine that refuses to recognize him as a true artist, never mind the genius he probably is.

​As a rapper, he is the antithetic to “polite society.” As a Black man, he is dangerous, misogynist, and violent. He isn’t perceived to be as “mainstream-friendly” as Jay-Z, nor does he fit so neatly into the “Socially Conscious Rapper Genre®™” as does Kendrick Lamar. President George W. Bush accidentally called him “Conway” multiple times (essentially proving Kanye’s point) and President Barack Obama, our cultural epitome of the Chill (But Successful) Black Guy, called him a “jackass.” The media has a tendency to take everything Kanye (and Nicki Minaj, and Lil Wayne, and basically every rapper) does — from his antics to his lyrics — at face value, as if a Black person and artist is incapable of deploying figurative speech.

So, when Kanye raps “I feel like me and Taylor might still have sex / Why? I made that bitch famous,” it enters the mainstream media meaning-making machine as a literal comment driven by aggression, ignorance, misogyny, and an unchecked, violent-Black-male-libido. How could a Black rapper like West be capable of irony? Or of humor? Or of self-deprecation? Or of making a layered, nuanced statement that may not mean what it appears to mean? How could Kanye West — God forbid — know exactly what he is doing?

But when John Lennon, a bonafide Genius White Guy, writes lyrics like “I’d rather see you dead, little girl / Than to be with another man […] You’d better run for your life if you can, little girl,” he continues to pass through the mainstream-media-legend–making-machine. His lyrics are treated as either “just a song,” or as a shining example of songwriting brilliance, a flawless usage of metaphor and hyperbole. It should be noted here that John Lennon has actually confessed to beating his wives and children.

Of course, when assessing media response, we have to control for historical moment and the existence of the internet. But while we choose to forget Lennon’s truly shocking crimes, West’s words spawn headlines like “Kanye West Apparently Thinks He Might Still Have Sex With Taylor Swift” (tagline: “and he thinks he made her famous, too”), “Kanye West faces backlash over Taylor Swift lyric attack,” and “Kim Kardashian Jealous Kanye West Wants To Have Sex With Taylor Swift, Struggling with Post-Baby Weight Gain?” Taylor Swift’s brother is outraged, her fans are outraged, her squad is outraged, and as a result, the media is outraged, too.

Then, in the most predictable upset of 2016, Taylor’s easily-digestible pop record beat out Kendrick Lamar’s latest masterpiece, a rap album which unapologetically tackles issues of mass incarceration, racism, mental illness and economic inequality for album of the year. And what did Swift use her Grammy acceptance speech for? A girl-power-manifesto-turned-shade aimed at Kanye West.

And yet, Swift (standing on the shoulders of a bevy of music-industry executives who’ve explained to her that “feminist” is now a profitable identity) and her squad were silent when West last month took an actual below-the-belt blow at his ex and real, practicing feminist activist, Amber Rose. Not that it’s Swift’s job to call out all misogyny, and certainly not that Rose needed white feminism’s help: Her inspired, perfectly-executedresponse was easily the best thing that’s been tweeted yet this year, perhaps this decade.

Let me be clear: I am not suggesting that Kanye should receive a “free pass” for inappropriate or sexist behavior because “he’s an artist.” I’m also not ignoring the fact that West has achieved major rap industry and crossover success. He is considered, certainly, to be among the best rappers alive by the zeitgeist, and I’m sure his legacy will continue long after he’s gone.

But that’s what’s so terrifying: even as the media loudly praises Kanye’s artistry and achievements, it continues to subtly — and not-so-subtly — deploy language and images that fuel racism, sexism, and tired stereotypes. Even though much of today’s media accounts for transparently sensationalist clickbait, this mode of operation is highly dangerous.

I am not interested in maligning American mainstream rappers as categorically misogynist, or violent, or superficial. And while we should always be aware of racism and sexism no matter whose Twitter fingers it spawns from, we cannot do so without first deconstructing the ways in which the music industry, mainstream media, and white supremacist capitalist patriarchy work in concert.
And of course, it is this very triumvirate that many rappers, including West, actively seek to threaten through their art.

Want to read more? Follow Julia Leonardos on Twitter @chopupthesoul. 

Julia Leonardos is an NYU-Gallatin student studying hip-hop/rap history, art, English and African literature, media, gender theory, and critical race theory.

When We Fight, We Win!

5/3/2016

0 Comments

 
​Same-sex marriage, #BlackLivesMatter, the DREAM Act, the People’s Climate March, End the New Jim Crow, Occupy Wall Street, the fight for a $15 minimum wage—these are just a few of the remarkable movements that have blossomed in the past decade, a most fertile and productive era of activism. Now, in a visually rich and deeply inspiring book, the leaders and activists of these and other movements distill their wisdom, sharing lessons of what makes—and what hinders—transformative social change.

Longtime social activist Greg Jobin-Leeds joins forces with AgitArte, a collective of artists and organizers, to capture the stories, philosophy, tactics, and art of today’s leading social change movements. When We Fight, We Win! weaves together interviews with today’s most successful activists and artists from across the country and beyond—including Patrisse Cullors-Brignac, Bill McKibben, Clayton Thomas-Muller, Karen Lewis, Favianna Rodriguez, Rea Carey, and Gaby Pacheco, among others—with narrative recountings of strategies and campaigns alongside full-color photos. It includes a foreword by Rinku Sen and an afterword by Antonia Darder.

When We Fight, We Win!: Twenty-First-Century Social Movements and the Activists That Are Transforming Our World will give a whole generation of readers the chance to celebrate and benefit from a remarkable decade of activism—a decade that shows just how ripe these times are for social transformation. It is a crucial addition to courses concerning sociology and the study of social movements, current affairs, art, and culture. 

​Greg Jobin-Leeds is co-chair of the Schott Foundation for Public Education. He has helped launch many high-impact social change organizations and lives in Cambridge, Massachusetts.

AgitArte creates projects and practices of cultural solidarity with workers and marginalized communities in grassroots struggles. José Jorge Diaz and José “Primo” Hernández are members of AgitArte, based in Santurce, Puerto Rico. Deymirie Hernández is a member of AgitArte, based in Boston, Massachusetts.

Paperback / $17.95 / 208 pages / ISBN 978-1-62097-093-5 / Published January 5, 2016
Also available as an e-book
